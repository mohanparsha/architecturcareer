The Master Sequence

No skill operates in isolation. Design Thinking prevents you from solving the wrong problem. Analytical Thinking ensures your solution is structurally sound. Critical Thinking acts as the continuous quality check — questioning assumptions, testing evidence, and exposing logical gaps throughout.

Used together: Design → Analytical → Critical produces outputs that are right for the user, structurally sound, and logically defensible — simultaneously. This is the combination that senior professionals, great consultants, and effective product leaders use instinctively.

Metacognition — The Invisible Layer

Beneath all three skills sits metacognition: thinking about your own thinking. Developing the habit of catching yourself mid-assumption, noticing circular reasoning, and recognising when a bias has entered your analysis is the single highest-leverage habit this guide builds. John Flavell (Stanford) identified metacognition as the foundational cognitive skill. It is Foundation #1 in this guide — and the theme running through every framework.

Prerequisites

10 Foundations Before the Frameworks

The 19 frameworks in this guide are powerful tools — but tools used without the right mental substrate produce poor results. These 10 prerequisites are the soil in which the frameworks take root. Invest time here first; return here when a framework feels shallow.

🏗 Estimated build time: 4–6 weeks at 30–45 min/day. Foundations deepen with every framework you apply — they are not a one-time study.
01

🧠 Metacognition

Thinking about your own thinking. The ability to monitor, evaluate, and regulate your reasoning in real time. John Flavell (Stanford) identified metacognition as the highest-leverage cognitive skill — more predictive of academic success than IQ.

  • Keep a daily 10-min "thinking journal" — what did I assume today?
  • After every decision, ask: what reasoning process did I use?
  • Read: Thinking, Fast and Slow (Kahneman) — Chs 1–5
⏱ 2–3 weeks of daily journaling
02

🔗 Logical Reasoning → enables FW03 Socratic Method, FW04 RED Model

Understanding the three inference modes: Deductive (certainty from premises), Inductive (probability from patterns), and Abductive (best explanation from incomplete data). Knowing which mode you're using — and its limits — is fundamental.

  • Classify today's news headlines as deductive, inductive, or abductive claims
  • Learn 15 common logical fallacies; identify one per day in real arguments
  • Resource: Khan Academy Logic & Reasoning (free)
⏱ 1–2 weeks
03

🎭 Cognitive Biases → underpins FW01 Paul-Elder, FW05 Six Hats, FW06 OODA Orient phase

Systematic errors in human thinking that distort decisions. You cannot eliminate biases — but you can name them, spot them in real time, and design processes that reduce their influence. Priority list: Confirmation Bias, Anchoring, Availability Heuristic, Dunning-Kruger Effect, Sunk Cost Fallacy, Halo Effect, Fundamental Attribution Error, In-Group Bias.

  • Build a personal "bias log" — note each bias you catch in your own thinking
  • For each major decision, name the 3 biases most threatening your reasoning
  • Resource: You Are Not So Smart (McRaney) — podcast + book
⏱ 2 weeks + ongoing vigilance
04

🌱 Growth Mindset

Carol Dweck's (Stanford) research shows that believing intelligence is developable — not fixed — predicts learning outcomes more than initial ability. A fixed mindset treats failure as a verdict. A growth mindset treats it as data. Thinking skill development requires repeated failure; the mindset determines whether you learn from it.

  • Reframe: "I'm bad at this" → "I haven't mastered this yet"
  • Keep an "error log" — for every thinking mistake, write what you'd do differently
  • Resource: Mindset (Carol Dweck)
⏱ Ongoing — cultivate daily
05

🔍 Information Literacy → required for FW01 Paul-Elder (Information element), FW04 RED Model

The ability to locate, evaluate, and use information effectively. Distinguishes primary sources (original research, firsthand accounts) from secondary sources (interpretations, summaries). Includes evaluating credibility, checking methodology, and recognising the difference between correlation and causation.

  • For any statistic you cite, trace it to the primary source
  • Apply SIFT: Stop — Investigate the source — Find better coverage — Trace claims
  • Resource: Web Literacy for Student Fact-Checkers (free, Caulfield)
⏱ 1 week + applied to every source
06

📊 Quantitative Literacy → required for FW11 SWOT+PESTEL, FW12 Systems Thinking, FW10 5 Whys data

The ability to reason with numbers: percentages, base rates, averages (mean vs. median), sample sizes, and confidence intervals. Fermi estimation — breaking unknowns into estimable components — is the most practical daily application. Without this, data-rich frameworks (SWOT, Systems Thinking) are used blindly.

  • Solve 5 Fermi estimation problems per week — e.g. "How many dentists in Mumbai?"
  • For any statistic: what is the base rate? What is the sample size?
  • Resource: Naked Statistics (Wheelan); Khan Academy Statistics (free)
⏱ 2–3 weeks of practice
07

♾️ Systems Awareness → prerequisite for FW12 Systems Thinking, FW13 Cynefin (Complex domain)

The intuition that everything is connected via feedback. Problems rarely have single, linear causes — they exist within systems with stocks, flows, reinforcing loops, and balancing loops. Before using Systems Thinking as a formal framework, you need this as an instinct: always ask "what feeds back where?"

  • For any current problem, draw a simple causal loop: what causes what, does it loop?
  • Identify one reinforcing loop (virtuous or vicious) in your own life or work
  • Resource: Thinking in Systems (Donella Meadows) — first 50 pages
⏱ 1–2 weeks
08

🎯 Intellectual Humility

Calibrated confidence: knowing what you know, what you don't know, and the difference. Intellectually humble thinkers update their views faster when evidence changes, steelman opposing arguments better, and forecast more accurately. This is not weakness — it is epistemic precision.

  • Assign probability ranges to your beliefs — e.g., "70% confident this is true"
  • For every strong opinion, write down the evidence that would change your mind
  • Resource: Superforecasting (Tetlock); fs.blog (free)
⏱ Ongoing — track your forecasting accuracy
09

👁️ Active Observation → prerequisite for FW14 d.school HCD (Empathise stage), FW15 Double Diamond (Discover)

Seeing what is actually there, not what you expect to see. Design Thinking's Empathise stage depends entirely on this skill — watching without immediately interpreting, asking without presuming the answer, listening without planning your response. Most poor design starts with failure to observe accurately.

  • Once per week: watch someone use a product — take 10 observations without evaluation
  • Practise "thick description": describe what you see in full detail before interpreting
  • Resource: IDEO Design Thinking Toolkit (free)
⏱ 2–3 weeks of deliberate practice
10

✍️ Writing as Thinking

Writing forces precision. Vague thinking produces vague writing; the discipline of articulating a claim in a sentence exposes every gap in the underlying reasoning. All 19 frameworks produce better outputs when thinking is done in writing. Start a Thinking Journal on Day 1 — never stop.

  • Write a 150-word summary of any argument you're working through — daily
  • After applying any framework, write a one-paragraph synthesis of your conclusions
  • Resource: Writing to Learn (Zinsser); On Writing Well (Zinsser)

01/19Paul-Elder FrameworkCritical Thinking

The most comprehensive critical thinking model — 8 Elements of Thought × 9 Intellectual Standards | Richard Paul & Linda Elder

▾
What It Is

Developed by Dr. Richard Paul and Dr. Linda Elder at the Foundation for Critical Thinking. The most academically rigorous critical thinking model. Every instance of thinking involves 8 Elements of Thought (Purpose, Question at Issue, Information, Inferences, Concepts, Assumptions, Implications, Point of View) — each evaluated against 9 Intellectual Standards: Clarity, Accuracy, Precision, Relevance, Depth, Breadth, Logic, Significance, and Fairness.

When To Use
  • Evaluating arguments in essays, reports, or debates
  • Analysing any recommendation before accepting it
  • Writing rigorous analytical content
  • Reviewing peers' work for logical quality
  • Any situation where the stakes of poor reasoning are high
How To Apply (8-Step Process)
  1. Purpose: What is the author/speaker trying to achieve?
  2. Question: What exact question is being addressed?
  3. Information: What data, evidence, or experience is used?
  4. Inference: What conclusions are drawn from the information?
  5. Concepts: What key ideas or theories are employed?
  6. Assumptions: What is taken for granted without proof?
  7. Implications: If the conclusion is accepted, what follows?
  8. Point of View: From what perspective is this argued?

Then apply the 9 standards: Is this reasoning clear, accurate, precise, relevant, deep, broad, logical, significant, and fair?

Real-World Example

Claim: "Remote work increases productivity."

  • Purpose: Justify a policy change
  • Question: Does remote work causally improve output?
  • Information: One company's survey data (single source)
  • Assumption: This company's experience generalises universally
  • Implication: Offices are unnecessary

Applying standards: fails on Precision (what type of work?), Depth (individual vs. collaborative?), and Fairness (disregards contrary evidence).

Practice Exercise
🎯 Today's Task — 20 min

Take any opinion article. Map all 8 Elements of Thought. Evaluate against 3 of the 9 standards. Write a 100-word critique. Focus especially on what is being assumed without proof.

02/19Bloom's Taxonomy (Revised)Critical Thinking

6-level cognitive hierarchy: Remember → Understand → Apply → Analyse → Evaluate → Create | Anderson & Krathwohl, 2001

▾
What It Is

Originally created by Benjamin Bloom (1956), revised by Anderson & Krathwohl (2001). A hierarchical model of cognitive skill depth. The key insight: each level requires mastery of all lower levels. Most academic work stops at Apply (level 3). The frameworks in this guide primarily operate at levels 4–6. Most people who think they understand something are actually operating at level 2.

The 6 Levels
  1. Remember: Recall facts and basic concepts
  2. Understand: Explain ideas in your own words
  3. Apply: Use in a new situation
  4. Analyse: Break down, compare, distinguish
  5. Evaluate: Judge validity, critique, defend
  6. Create: Produce new work, combine, design
How To Use as a Self-Diagnostic

For any concept or framework you claim to know, honestly answer one question per level:

  1. Can you define it from memory?
  2. Can you explain it with an original example?
  3. Can you apply it to a brand-new problem?
  4. Can you compare it to alternatives and identify its limits?
  5. Can you judge whether it should be used in a specific context?
  6. Can you create something new using it?

Where you first answer "no" is your current level. That is precisely where to direct your practice.

Real-World Example

Testing understanding of the Cynefin framework:

  • L1: Define the 5 domains
  • L2: Explain why Complex ≠ Complicated with an example
  • L3: Classify today's 3 biggest problems into domains
  • L4: Compare Cynefin vs. SWOT for strategic decisions
  • L5: Judge when Cynefin adds value vs. creates confusion
  • L6: Write a one-page Cynefin guide for your team
Practice Exercise
🎯 Today's Task — 15 min

Pick any framework in this guide. Write one concrete task for each of the 6 levels. If you struggle at level 4+, that is your exact learning gap. Spend the next week only on those levels.

03/19Socratic MethodCritical Thinking

Disciplined questioning to expose hidden assumptions — 6 question categories | Socrates (via Plato)

▾
What It Is

Named after the Greek philosopher Socrates, documented by Plato. A structured inquiry method using six categories of disciplined questions to expose assumptions, probe evidence, and arrive at well-examined conclusions. The foundational method of Western intellectual tradition — still used in law schools (the "Socratic method") and leading business schools globally.

The 6 Question Categories
  1. Conceptual Clarification: "What exactly do you mean by X?"
  2. Probing Assumptions: "What are you assuming? Is that always true?"
  3. Probing Evidence: "What is the evidence? How was it obtained?"
  4. Questioning Viewpoints: "What would a critic say?"
  5. Exploring Implications: "If this is true, what follows?"
  6. Questioning the Question: "Why is this the right question to ask?"
How To Apply

Use as a self-dialogue on any claim or belief you hold. Work through each category systematically — do not skip Probing Assumptions. Most beliefs are never questioned at this level and collapse quickly under even gentle Socratic inquiry.

In group settings: assign one person to each question category. Rotate. This prevents the common failure of one person dominating "devil's advocate" while others remain passive.

When To Use
  • Examining your own strongly held beliefs
  • Evaluating a recommendation before acting on it
  • Tutorial discussions, seminars, and case analyses
  • Breaking through intellectual deadlocks in teams
  • Preparing for debates or presentations where challenge is expected
Real-World Example

Claim: "Social media is damaging mental health."

  • Clarify: What does "damaging" mean — clinical diagnosis or subjective report?
  • Assumptions: That causation (not correlation) has been established
  • Evidence: Which studies? What controls? What sample sizes?
  • Viewpoints: What do proponents of digital connection argue?
  • Implications: Should platforms be regulated? Which features specifically?
  • Q the Q: Are we asking about all social media, or specific uses?
Practice Exercise
🎯 Today's Task — 15 min

Write down one strongly held belief. Apply all 6 Socratic question categories. Answer each honestly. If the belief survives rigorous inquiry, it is now more defensible. If it doesn't — update it.

04/19RED ModelCritical Thinking

Recognise → Evaluate → Draw | Pearson TalentLens / Watson-Glaser Critical Thinking Appraisal

▾
What It Is

Developed by Pearson TalentLens as the framework underlying the Watson-Glaser Critical Thinking Appraisal — the world's most widely used critical thinking assessment, deployed by law firms, consulting firms, and major employers globally. RED stands for: Recognise Assumptions, Evaluate Arguments, Draw Conclusions. Its simplicity makes it deployable in under 60 seconds on any argument.

When To Use
  • Reading proposals, reports, recommendations quickly
  • Evaluating arguments under time pressure
  • Any professional situation requiring structured critical analysis
  • Preparation for aptitude assessments (Watson-Glaser, SHL, etc.)
  • Graduate-level essay and case analysis
How To Apply
  1. Recognise assumptions: List every unstated assumption the argument depends upon. Ask: "What must be true for this argument to hold?" Focus on the most consequential assumptions — the ones where, if false, the entire argument fails.
  2. Evaluate arguments: For each argument, ask — Is it relevant? Is it strong? Is it supported by evidence? Does it commit a fallacy? Is the evidence primary or secondary? Is there selection bias in what evidence is presented?
  3. Draw conclusions: Form a conclusion that follows necessarily from available evidence — not from preference or analogy. Qualify your conclusion appropriately: "The evidence supports X but does not rule out Y."
Real-World Example

Claim: "Sales are down because our prices are too high."

  • Recognise: Assumes price is the dominant factor (not quality, competition, market change, distribution, or salesforce effectiveness)
  • Evaluate: Is there direct price-sensitivity data? Was a controlled test run? Do competitor pricing data support this? Is this argument relevant and strong?
  • Draw: Insufficient evidence to conclude price alone is causal. Correct conclusion: "Price is a hypothesis to test alongside 4 others."
Practice Exercise
🎯 Today's Task — 10 min

Find any business article with a recommendation. Apply RED: list 3 assumptions, evaluate 2 arguments for strength, write your independent conclusion in 50 words. Note specifically whether the conclusion follows necessarily from the evidence, or is a leap.

05/19Six Thinking HatsCritical Thinking

6 parallel cognitive modes — White Facts, Red Emotions, Black Caution, Yellow Benefits, Green Creativity, Blue Process | Dr. Edward de Bono, 1985

▾
What It Is

Created by Dr. Edward de Bono (1985). The framework separates thinking into six distinct, non-overlapping cognitive modes — preventing the common failure of mixing emotional, analytical, and creative thinking simultaneously, which produces circular arguments and muddled decisions. Used by major corporations, governments, and in Harvard Business Review as a decision-making standard.

The 6 Hats
  • 🤍 White: Facts, data, and objective information only
  • ❤️ Red: Emotions, gut feelings — no justification required
  • 🖤 Black: Risks, cautions, and potential failures
  • 💛 Yellow: Benefits, optimism, value
  • 💚 Green: Creative alternatives and new possibilities
  • 💙 Blue: Process management — opens and closes every session
How To Apply
  1. Blue (Open): Define topic. Agree on the sequence of hats.
  2. White: Lay out all known facts. No opinions allowed.
  3. Red: Each person shares gut reaction. No justification needed.
  4. Black: Spend 5 minutes minimum on risks. Be rigorous and pessimistic.
  5. Yellow: List every genuine benefit. Be constructively optimistic.
  6. Green: Generate creative alternatives. Suspend judgement entirely.
  7. Blue (Close): Summarise insights and identify the decision or next step.

Tip: Black hat is the most skipped in optimistic teams — and the most valuable. Protect its time.

Real-World Example

Decision: Should I apply for a new role at a different company?

  • ⬜ White: Salary +20%, role is broader, commute +40 min/day, company has 3-year funding runway
  • 🔴 Red: Excited but anxious about leaving familiar team
  • ⬛ Black: Risk of culture mismatch; startup could fail; lose current seniority
  • 🟡 Yellow: Faster career growth, new skills, broader network
  • 🟢 Green: Could negotiate a 3-month trial? Part-time consulting first?
Practice Exercise
🎯 Today's Task — 25 min

Apply all 6 hats to a decision you are currently weighing. Spend 3 minutes per hat. Write what emerged under Black (risks) and Green (alternatives) that you had not previously considered. These two hats consistently produce the highest-value insights.

06/19OODA LoopCritical Thinking

Observe → Orient → Decide → Act — Colonel John Boyd, USAF

▾
What It Is

Developed by USAF Colonel John Boyd from his study of fighter pilot decision cycles. Boyd's critical insight: the Orient phase is not merely one step — it is the cognitive framework through which all observations are filtered. Your mental models, experience, cultural tradition, and biases all live inside Orient. Whoever loops faster — not who is most "correct" — wins in competitive environments. Now widely used in business strategy and product development.

The 4 Phases
  1. Observe: Gather raw, unfiltered data. Resist premature interpretation.
  2. Orient: Filter through mental models. Critically examine: which model am I using? Is it still accurate?
  3. Decide: Select a course of action from interpreted data.
  4. Act: Execute — which generates new observations for the next loop.
How To Apply

The most important diagnostic question in OODA is: "Is my Orient model still current?"

Many strategic mistakes occur not in Observe or Decide — but in Orient. People continue applying a mental model formed 5 years ago to a situation that has fundamentally changed. OODA applied with rigour means explicitly auditing your Orient models.

When To Use
  • Fast-moving competitive environments
  • Iterative product and strategy decisions
  • Understanding why your decisions feel slow
  • Post-mortems on strategic failures
Real-World Example

Startup responding to a competitor's feature launch:

  • Observe: The feature, user reactions, competitor timing
  • Orient: Through your understanding of your users' jobs-to-be-done — does this feature address a job your users have? What mental model are you using about your competitive advantage?
  • Decide: Copy, differentiate, or ignore
  • Act: Ship a response — which your competitor now observes

The team that loops in 2 weeks beats the team looping in 2 months, regardless of individual decision quality.

Practice Exercise
🎯 Today's Task — 10 min

Replay a recent decision. Map it to OODA. Focus on Orient: what mental model were you using? Was it current and accurate? Write 3 sentences on what you would have oriented differently — and what that would have changed.

07/19MECE + Issue TreesAnalytical Thinking

Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive — Barbara Minto, McKinsey & Company

▾
What It Is

MECE was developed by Barbara Minto at McKinsey in the 1960s–70s. It is the foundational principle of structured analytical thinking. When you decompose a problem, categories must not overlap (Mutually Exclusive) and together must cover every possibility (Collectively Exhaustive). Issue Trees (Logic Trees) are the visual tool: WHY trees find root causes; HOW trees find solutions.

The MECE Test
  • ME test: If item X is in branch A, can it also be in branch B? If yes — not ME. Restructure.
  • CE test: Do all branches together account for 100% of the problem space? If there are gaps — not CE. Add a branch.
How To Apply (5 Steps)
  1. State the problem as a single specific question
  2. Branch level 1: Identify 2–4 MECE categories. Test: ME? CE?
  3. Branch level 2: Break each branch further. Apply MECE test again.
  4. Branch level 3: Continue until branches are specific enough to be actionable hypotheses
  5. Validate: Walk the full tree. Does every leaf belong to exactly one branch? Do all leaves together cover the complete problem?

Common MECE structures: by stakeholder, by time phase, by geography, by value chain stage, by customer segment.

Real-World Example

Problem: "Why are my exam scores declining?"

Level 1 (MECE): Input quality | Study process | Assessment format

Study process branch: Time spent | Study technique | Retrieval practice | Environment

Check: Do any overlap? (No) Do all together = 100% of score variation? (Yes — validated).

Now apply 5 Whys to the highest-probability branch identified by data.

Practice Exercise
🎯 Today's Task — 15 min

Pick any current problem. Draw a 3-level Issue Tree on paper. After completing it, run the MECE test: circle any overlap, mark any gap. If you find either, restructure that branch before continuing. Do this on paper first — it reveals gaps that screen-based tools hide.

08/19Pyramid PrincipleAnalytical Thinking

Answer first, support second — SCR communication structure | Barbara Minto, McKinsey

▾
What It Is

Created by Barbara Minto at McKinsey in the 1970s and documented in The Pyramid Principle (1987). The core insight: effective analytical communication always leads with the answer, not builds toward it. Structure: Top-level answer (recommendation) → 3 supporting arguments (MECE) → evidence under each. The SCR template provides the narrative frame: Situation–Complication–Resolution.

SCR Template
  • Situation: The known context everyone agrees on
  • Complication: The tension, change, or problem that disrupts the situation
  • Resolution: Your recommendation — stated in the first sentence
How To Apply
  1. Draft SCR in one paragraph. This forces you to know your recommendation before you start writing. If you can't state it clearly — your analysis is incomplete.
  2. State the answer first in every communication. Never bury it.
  3. Identify 3 supporting arguments. Apply MECE test: no overlap, no gaps.
  4. Support each argument with specific evidence, data, or examples.
  5. Validate: Does each argument directly and exclusively support the top answer? Would removing any argument weaken the case?
Real-World Example

SCR: Our product retention is 60% (S). Users churn in month 2 due to a specific onboarding gap (C). We should rebuild the onboarding flow focused on the activation moment (R).

3 supporting arguments:

  1. Month-2 churn data confirms this pattern (with data)
  2. User interviews identify the exact feature gap (with quotes)
  3. Two competitors who fixed this saw 40% retention improvement (evidence)
Practice Exercise
🎯 Today's Task — 15 min

Take the last email you wrote where you built to a conclusion at the end. Rewrite it: SCR in paragraph one, recommendation in the first sentence. Show both versions to a colleague. Ask which they would act on faster. This exercise — done once — changes your written communication permanently.

09/19First Principles ThinkingAnalytical Thinking

Strip to irreducible truths, reason upward | Aristotle → Elon Musk / SpaceX

▾
What It Is

Attributed to Aristotle but made famous in modern business by Elon Musk. First principles thinking means breaking a problem to its fundamental, irreducible truths — stripping away every assumption, analogy, and convention — then reasoning upward from those truths to build novel solutions. The opposite is analogical reasoning: "others do it this way, so we should too." Most innovation failures are failures of analogical reasoning mistaken for strategic thinking.

When To Use
  • Innovation — finding solutions others have missed
  • Cost reduction — challenging why something must cost what it does
  • Challenging industry conventions never examined
  • When existing solutions consistently disappoint
How To Apply (5 Steps)
  1. Define the problem specifically and concretely
  2. List all current assumptions — write every "fact" you take as given
  3. Test each assumption: Is this fundamentally true? Or is it convention, analogy, or habit?
  4. Identify the irreducible fundamentals — what must be true regardless of how the solution is built? (Physics, mathematics, human nature)
  5. Reason upward: Build a solution exclusively from verified fundamentals
Real-World Example

Musk on rocket manufacturing costs:

  • Convention: "Rockets cost $65M — that's just what they cost"
  • First principles: What are rockets made of? Aerospace aluminium, titanium, copper, carbon fibre
  • Market price of those materials: ~$2M
  • Conclusion: The cost of a rocket should be ~3% of market price
  • Result: SpaceX achieved exactly this

The conventional assumption was convention. The first principle was physics. The gap between them was the innovation opportunity.

Practice Exercise
🎯 Today's Task — 20 min

Name one thing in your field "everyone knows" is done a certain way. List every assumption behind it. Test each: is this convention or physical/logical necessity? What solution would you build from the ground up, starting only from what must be fundamentally true?

10/195 Whys + Fishbone DiagramAnalytical Thinking

Root cause analysis | Sakichi Toyoda (5 Whys) + Kaoru Ishikawa (Fishbone) — Toyota Production System

▾
What It Is

The 5 Whys was developed by Sakichi Toyoda and is foundational to the Toyota Production System. The Fishbone (Ishikawa) Diagram, created by Kaoru Ishikawa, maps causes across the 6M categories: Man, Machine, Material, Method, Measurement, Mother Nature (in services: People, Process, Place, Policies, Procedures, Perceptions). Use Fishbone to map the full cause landscape; 5 Whys to drill into the highest-probability branch.

When To Use
  • Quality problems and process failures
  • Academic or professional post-mortems
  • Repeated problems that previous "solutions" haven't fixed
  • Any situation where acting on the symptom would be wasteful or harmful
How To Apply (5 Steps)
  1. State the problem clearly. Avoid vague problem statements — "poor performance" is not a problem statement; "Exam score dropped 15 points in 4 weeks" is.
  2. Fishbone first (for complex problems): Map all potential causes across 6M on a fishbone diagram
  3. Select the highest-probability branch from the Fishbone based on data or observation
  4. Apply 5 Whys: Ask "Why?" repeatedly until you reach an actionable root cause. Typically 4–6 iterations. Stop when you reach something you can actually fix.
  5. Validate: If you fix this root cause, will the problem disappear permanently? If fixing a symptom instead — it will recur.
Real-World Example

Problem: Exam score was significantly below expectations.

  1. Why? Didn't understand the material in the exam
  2. Why? Spent too little time on the right topics
  3. Why? No structured study plan — studied what felt familiar
  4. Why? Never diagnosed which topics were weak
  5. Why? Never used past papers to identify knowledge gaps

Root cause: Absence of a diagnostic-led study approach. Fix: Practice papers first, then study gaps — not the other way round.

Practice Exercise
🎯 Today's Task — 15 min

Take any recent setback. Apply 5 Whys — write each answer down. Stop when you reach something genuinely actionable. If you reached the root cause by Why 2, you've likely stopped at a symptom. Push further until you find something that, if fixed, makes the problem structurally impossible to recur.

11/19SWOT + PESTELAnalytical Thinking

Integrated strategic analysis — internal capabilities + macro-environment scan | Albert Humphrey, Stanford Research Institute (SWOT, 1960s) + Francis Aguilar (PEST, 1967)

▾
What It Is

SWOT (Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, Threats) for internal and competitive analysis. PESTEL (Political, Economic, Social, Technological, Environmental, Legal) for macro-environment scanning. Used together: PESTEL generates the external landscape that feeds directly into SWOT's Opportunities and Threats. Without PESTEL, SWOT's OT analysis is shallow and present-biased — the most common SWOT failure.

When To Use
  • Career planning and professional positioning
  • Business strategy and new venture evaluation
  • Any decision with significant internal and external dimensions
  • Annual or quarterly strategic reviews
How To Apply (6 Steps)
  1. PESTEL scan: For each of 6 macro-factors, identify 2–3 relevant trends with evidence
  2. Extract O&T from PESTEL: Which trends are opportunities? Which are threats?
  3. Internal assessment: List Strengths and Weaknesses honestly — get external input; we consistently overestimate our strengths
  4. SO strategy: How do your strengths exploit the identified opportunities?
  5. ST strategy: How do your strengths counter the identified threats?
  6. WO/WT strategies: Address weaknesses to capture opportunities; minimise exposure to threats
Real-World Example

Student career planning with SWOT+PESTEL:

PESTEL reveals: AI growth (T: automation risk for routine analytical roles; O: new AI-adjacent roles requiring human judgement). Strengths: strong domain knowledge, analytical skills. Weakness: limited professional network in target industry. Strategy (WO): build the network specifically in AI-adjacent roles now, before those positions become highly competitive. Without PESTEL, this student focuses on traditional roles now at risk of automation.

Practice Exercise
🎯 Today's Task — 30 min

Apply SWOT+PESTEL to your own career development over the next 3 years. Be ruthlessly honest about Weaknesses — ask a trusted peer to review your list. The highest-value output is always in the WO cell: opportunities you could seize if you addressed a specific weakness.

12/19Systems ThinkingAnalytical Thinking

Stocks, flows & feedback loops | Jay Forrester (MIT) · Peter Senge, The Fifth Discipline

▾
What It Is

Developed at MIT by Jay Forrester (System Dynamics Group) and popularised by Peter Senge in The Fifth Discipline (1990). Key concepts: stocks (accumulated quantities), flows (rates of change), reinforcing loops (amplify change — virtuous or vicious cycles), balancing loops (resist change — regulate toward equilibrium), and delays (the primary cause of counterintuitive, unintended system behaviour).

The Critical Insight

Most problems that "keep coming back" despite solutions are system problems, not event problems. You fixed the symptom (the event) but left the structure (the system) intact. Systems thinking makes the structure visible.

How To Apply (6 Steps)
  1. Map key stocks (what accumulates: skills, users, inventory, trust, stress)
  2. Map flows in and out of each stock
  3. Identify reinforcing loops (R) — where does growth feed more growth?
  4. Identify balancing loops (B) — where does growth trigger resistance?
  5. Mark delays — time lags between cause and effect
  6. Find leverage points — where does a small change produce large, lasting effects?

Donella Meadows: the highest leverage point is changing the goal of the system — not the flows.

Real-World Example

Academic performance system:

  • Stock: Knowledge, confidence, stress level
  • Reinforcing (R): Better understanding → higher confidence → more engagement → even better understanding (virtuous)
  • Balancing (B): Increasing workload → increasing stress → reduced sleep → reduced cognitive performance → lower scores (resistance)
  • Delay: Effort invested in week 1 improves scores in week 6 — the delay causes students to underinvest
  • Leverage: Sleep quality — affects both the stress balancing loop and the knowledge reinforcing loop simultaneously
Practice Exercise
🎯 Today's Task — 20 min

Map any persistent problem in your life as a system. Identify one reinforcing loop and one balancing loop. Mark the longest delay. Now ask: what is the highest-leverage intervention — the one that changes the structure, not just the immediate symptoms?

13/19Cynefin FrameworkAnalytical Thinking

Match your thinking approach to the problem domain | Dave Snowden, IBM (1999) — pronounced "kuh-NEV-in" (Welsh: habitat)

▾
What It Is

Developed by Dave Snowden at IBM in 1999. Cynefin is a sense-making framework that classifies problems into five domains, each requiring a different decision-making approach. The most dangerous mistake in strategic thinking is applying Complicated tools (analytical best practice) to Complex situations — this is the root cause of many failed change programmes, product launches, and strategies.

The 5 Domains
  • ✅ Clear: Cause-effect known. Best practice. Sense-Categorise-Respond. (Renamed from "Simple" by Snowden in 2020 — older sources may use the previous term.)
  • 🔧 Complicated: Cause-effect discoverable by experts. Good practice. Sense-Analyse-Respond.
  • 🌊 Complex: Cause-effect only knowable in retrospect. Emergent practice. Probe-Sense-Respond.
  • 🔥 Chaotic: No cause-effect relationship. Novel practice. Act-Sense-Respond.
  • ❓ Disorder: Domain unknown. Get out immediately.
How To Apply
  1. Diagnose the domain first. Before selecting any framework or tool, ask: which domain does this problem live in?
  2. For Clear: Apply best practice — standardise and automate
  3. For Complicated: Bring in expertise — analyse then act
  4. For Complex: Run multiple small safe-to-fail experiments — don't analyse first; probe first
  5. For Chaotic: Act immediately to establish order — don't wait for analysis
  6. Critical warning: Never use MECE, SWOT, or any structured analytical framework for Complex problems — they impose false certainty
Real-World Example

Student examples across domains:

  • Clear: Formatting a bibliography — rules are known, follow them
  • Complicated: Understanding a complex statistical method — needs expert teaching, analysis
  • Complex: Predicting which career field will thrive in 10 years — cannot be analysed, must be explored with small bets
  • Chaotic: Mental health crisis — act immediately, don't analyse first

The Cynefin test: if you're using a structured analytical framework but the situation keeps surprising you — you're probably in Complex, not Complicated.

Practice Exercise
🎯 Today's Task — 10 min

List your 5 most pressing current decisions or problems. Classify each into a Cynefin domain. For any you classified as Complex that you've been trying to analyse — stop. Instead, design one small, safe-to-fail experiment you could run this week.

💡 Design Thinking frameworks are your discovery and creation toolkit — they ensure you are solving the right problem for real people before committing analytical resources. Always deploy DT before AT, not after.
14/19Stanford d.school HCDDesign Thinking

Empathise → Define → Ideate → Prototype → Test | Stanford d.school Human-Centred Design

▾
What It Is

Developed by the Stanford d.school (Hasso Plattner Institute of Design) as the canonical five-stage Design Thinking process. Used by IDEO, major corporations, and design schools globally. The critical distinction from other problem-solving methods: it is non-linear and iterative — you are expected to loop back at any point based on what you learn. The Empathise stage is the most skipped and the most essential.

The 5 Stages
  1. Empathise: Observe, engage, and immerse in users' experiences. Watch, listen, ask. Never skip this.
  2. Define: Synthesise observations into a POV statement and HMW questions
  3. Ideate: Generate ideas — quantity first, quality later. Aim for 20+ before evaluating.
  4. Prototype: Build the cheapest, fastest version of your top ideas
  5. Test: Put prototypes in front of real users. Learn, iterate, return to any previous stage.
Key Tools Within This Framework

POV Statement: "[User] needs [need] because [insight]." The "because" forces a deep motivation, not a surface request.

HMW Questions: "How Might We..." — converts the POV into generative design questions. Generate 20+ before evaluating. "How" assumes solutions exist. "Might" removes pressure. "We" invites collaboration.

Rapid Prototyping: The prototype exists to learn, not to present. It should be built in hours, not days.

Real-World Example

Redesigning a university library study space:

  • Empathise: Observe students at different times of day; interview 10 students about their study challenges
  • Define POV: "Final-year students need flexible, low-distraction zones because they shift between deep individual work and brief collaborative sessions throughout a study day"
  • HMW: "How might we make it easy to transition between solo and collaborative modes without losing your space?"
  • Ideate: 25 ideas generated; top 3 selected for prototyping
  • Prototype: Cardboard furniture mockups, tested in an afternoon
Practice Exercise
🎯 Today's Task — 45 min

Pick any product or service you use daily that frustrates you. Spend 20 min observing yourself and others using it (Empathise). Write a POV statement. Generate 10 HMW questions. Select the most promising one. Sketch a 5-minute paper prototype of a solution. This full cycle in 45 minutes is the core skill.

15/19Double DiamondDesign Thinking

Discover → Define → Develop → Deliver — 2 diverge-converge cycles | UK Design Council, 2005 (updated 2019)

▾
What It Is

Developed and published by the UK Design Council in 2005, updated in 2019. The Double Diamond makes explicit that good design requires two separate phases of divergent thinking followed by convergent thinking. The first diamond defines the right problem; the second develops the right solution. The most common failure is skipping the first diamond entirely — jumping straight to solutions without discovering whether you've framed the problem correctly.

The 4 Phases
  1. Discover (diverge): Explore the problem space widely — research, observe, gather, learn
  2. Define (converge): Synthesise insights into a specific, validated problem statement
  3. Develop (diverge): Generate potential solutions — breadth before depth
  4. Deliver (converge): Test, refine, and release the solution
How To Apply

The Double Diamond's key discipline is honouring the diverge phase before the converge phase in each diamond. Most organisations under time pressure skip divergence — this produces precise answers to wrong questions.

Rule: You cannot enter the Develop phase until you have a validated, user-confirmed problem statement from the Define phase. If you're already developing solutions before you've defined the problem — you're in the wrong diamond.

2019 update added three design principles: Be people-centred, communicate visually, collaborate and co-create.

Real-World Example

University app redesign:

  • Discover: 30 student interviews, app usage analytics, competitor review — wide exploration
  • Define: "Students miss deadlines not because they forget them, but because they don't understand how each deadline connects to their grade" — specific, validated
  • Develop: 15 solution concepts generated; 4 prototyped
  • Deliver: One solution (contextual deadline reminders with grade impact) tested, refined, released

Without the first diamond, the team would have built a better notification system — solving the wrong problem.

Practice Exercise
🎯 Today's Task — 30 min

For any project you're currently working on: honestly assess which diamond you are in. If you are in Develop but skipped the Define phase — stop. Write your current problem statement. Can you validate it with 3 user observations? If not, you're still in Discover.

16/19IBM Enterprise Design ThinkingDesign Thinking

Hills + Playbacks + Sponsor Users — Design Thinking at organisational scale | IBM, 2012

▾
What It Is

IBM's adaptation of Design Thinking for large enterprise teams, developed from 2012. Three core practices that adapt DT to organisational constraints: Hills (outcome statements for alignment), Playbacks (regular stakeholder alignment sessions), and Sponsor Users (embedded real users throughout development). IBM trained 100,000+ employees in this framework and measured a 75% reduction in design-to-market time.

The Three Keys
  • Hills: Statements of intent for users in the format WHO + WHAT + WOW (the measurable outcome). Hills align teams around user outcomes, not features.
  • Playbacks: Regular internal demonstrations to stakeholders — not status reports. Test whether the team is aligned on the hill.
  • Sponsor Users: Real users embedded in the team who are available to provide continuous feedback throughout development.
How To Write a Hill

Format: [WHO] can [WHAT] so that [WOW measurable outcome].

Hills are not features — they are user outcomes. A good Hill makes no reference to technology or implementation.

Bad Hill (feature): "We will build a dashboard with real-time data."

Good Hill (outcome): "A first-year student can identify their three biggest study gaps in under 5 minutes, so that they know exactly what to study next without guessing."

Hills are testable. You can always ask: did the user achieve this outcome?

Real-World Example

Three Hills for a career guidance app:

  1. "A final-year student can map their current skills to target roles in 10 minutes, without needing a career counsellor appointment."
  2. "An early-career professional can identify the single most high-leverage skill to develop next, based on their goals and current market data."
  3. "A career coach can review 30 student profiles in the time currently spent on 10, without losing personalisation quality."
Practice Exercise
🎯 Today's Task — 15 min

Write 3 Hills for any project you are working on — academic or professional. Test each: Does it name a specific user? Does it describe an outcome (not a feature)? Does it include a measurable WOW condition? If you wrote features instead of outcomes, rewrite until the Hill is entirely about the user's experience.

17/19GV Design SprintDesign Thinking

5-day structured sprint to prototype and validate in one week | Jake Knapp, Google Ventures

▾
What It Is

Created by Jake Knapp at Google Ventures (GV) and documented in Sprint (2016). A 5-day structured process to answer critical business questions through design, prototyping, and testing with real users — before committing to full development. Research shows 5 user tests reveal ~80% of usability issues (Nielsen & Landauer, 1993). The sprint compresses months of decision-making into one week.

The 5-Day Structure
  1. Monday — Map: Define the challenge, map the user journey, choose a target
  2. Tuesday — Sketch: Individual solution sketching (Crazy 8s: 8 ideas in 8 minutes)
  3. Wednesday — Decide: Vote on the best solution, build a storyboard
  4. Thursday — Prototype: Build a realistic-looking prototype (not functional)
  5. Friday — Test: Interview 5 users with the prototype. This is enough.
How To Adapt for Students

A full 5-day GV Sprint requires a dedicated team. For students and early-career professionals, the "5-hour sprint" works for most academic projects:

  • Hour 1: Map & choose target (40 min) + Long-term goal (20 min)
  • Hour 2: Crazy 8s ideation (8 min) + Solution sketch (30 min) + Decide (22 min)
  • Hour 3: Prototype (paper/Figma/slides)
  • Hours 4–5: Test with 3–5 peers. Capture verbatim quotes.

Crazy 8s: Fold paper into 8. Sketch 8 distinct ideas in 8 minutes — 1 minute per panel. Forces quantity over quality. The 8th idea is usually the most original.

Real-World Example

Sprint for a new campus study-skills workshop format:

  • M: Target = "First-year student who has never attended a study-skills session"
  • T: 8 workshop format concepts sketched individually
  • W: Voted on "peer-led case study" format; storyboard built
  • T: Prototype session deck built in 4 hours (slides, not real session)
  • F: Tested with 5 first-years; 3 key insights emerged that changed the format

Cost: 5 days. Value: avoided building a full workshop that the target audience would not have attended.

Practice Exercise
🎯 Today's Task — 60 min

Run a mini-sprint on any current project. Spend 10 min on Crazy 8s — set a timer, sketch 8 ideas in 8 minutes, no evaluation. Then select the best one, sketch a 3-step user journey for it, and test it with one person. Write what surprised you.

18/19SCAMPERDesign Thinking

7 creative lenses for systematic ideation | Bob Eberle, 1971 (extending Alex Osborn)

▾
What It Is

Developed by Bob Eberle (1971) as an expansion of Alex Osborn's brainstorming checklist. SCAMPER provides seven structured lenses for generating ideas by applying deliberate transformations to an existing product, process, or system. It prevents the most common ideation failure: generating variations of the same idea in different words. Each lens forces a genuinely different generative perspective.

The 7 Lenses
  • 🔄 Substitute: What components could be replaced?
  • 🔗 Combine: What could be merged or integrated?
  • 🔧 Adapt: What from another domain could be applied?
  • ✏️ Modify/Magnify: What could be changed in scale, shape, or form?
  • 🎯 Put to other uses: How else could this be used?
  • ➖ Eliminate: What is unnecessary and can be removed?
  • ↩️ Reverse/Rearrange: What if the order or direction was reversed?
How To Apply

Use SCAMPER in the Ideate phase of any Design Thinking process, or when an ideation session has stalled and ideas are getting repetitive.

  1. Define the product, process, or problem clearly
  2. Work through each of the 7 lenses in sequence — spend 2 minutes per lens
  3. For each lens, generate at least 3 ideas before moving on
  4. Do not evaluate during generation — pure divergence first
  5. After completing all 7, evaluate the full set and cluster promising ideas

Total time: 14 minutes of pure generation produces 21+ ideas from a single input. Use before jumping to evaluation.

Real-World Example

SCAMPER applied to university lectures:

  • S: Substitute the lecturer with a student-taught peer session
  • C: Combine lecture + workshop into one format (flipped classroom)
  • A: Adapt Spotify's "Discovery Weekly" algorithm to personalise reading lists
  • M: Magnify Q&A time from 10 to 40 minutes; reduce lecture to 20 min
  • P: Put the lecture recording to use as pre-work, not the event itself
  • E: Eliminate slides entirely — teach from the whiteboard only
  • R: Reverse: students teach the concept back to the lecturer
Practice Exercise
🎯 Today's Task — 14 min

Pick any product, service, or process you interact with daily. Set a 2-minute timer per lens. Generate 3 ideas per lens without stopping to evaluate. After all 7 lenses, review the 21 ideas and circle the 3 that seem most genuinely original — not just variations of what already exists.

19/19Jobs-To-Be-Done (JTBD)Design Thinking

Design for the job, not the user | Tony Ulwick (1990) · Clayton Christensen (1999–2016) · Bob Moesta

▾
What It Is

Originated by Tony Ulwick (1990), developed and popularised by Clayton Christensen (Harvard Business School, 1999–2016), and further refined by Bob Moesta on the demand side. The core insight: people don't buy products — they hire them to do a job. Understanding the job-to-be-done more precisely than anyone else is the most reliable source of innovation. JTBD focuses design on the underlying motivation (the job), not the user's stated preferences or demographic profile.

Three Job Types
  • Functional: The practical task — "get me from A to B"
  • Emotional: How the user wants to feel — "feel safe and in control"
  • Social: How they want to be perceived — "look like a competent professional"

All three must be addressed. Most products address only the functional job — this is why they remain commodities.

How To Apply

Job Statement Format: [Verb] + [Object] + [Contextual clarifier]

Example: "Minimise the time required to identify my most critical study gap when preparing for exams under time pressure."

  1. Interview users about past behaviour, not future desires. "When did you last...?" not "Would you like...?"
  2. Map the full job — what are they trying to accomplish before, during, and after?
  3. Write 3 job statements (functional, emotional, social) for your primary user
  4. Design against each job statement — does your solution address all three?
  5. Validate: Ask users if your solution does the job better than alternatives
Real-World Example

Christensen's milkshake study (classic JTBD example):

A fast food chain was trying to improve milkshake sales. Demographic profiling and preference surveys failed. JTBD research found: commuters buy milkshakes at 8am — not because they like milkshakes, but because they need something to occupy their hand during a 40-minute commute that doesn't make a mess. The job: "entertain me during a boring commute while keeping me full until lunch." Competitors were bananas (too messy), bagels (too dry), and coffee (too hot). The milkshake won the job. Improved the viscosity — sales rose 7×.

Practice Exercise
🎯 Today's Task — 20 min

For any product or service you are designing or studying: write 3 job statements (functional, emotional, social) for the primary user. Then ask: does the current solution address all 3? Which job is underserved? That underserved job is your innovation opportunity. This is how Airbnb, Uber, and Slack found their wedge.

Step 1 — Design Thinking
Discover the right problem

Frameworks used: d.school HCD (Empathise + Define)

POV Statement: "A final-year student who is investing more time needs to understand why effort isn't translating to results, because the disconnect is eroding confidence and creating a self-defeating anxiety loop."

HMW Questions generated: How might we diagnose the quality (not quantity) of study time? How might we distinguish passive re-reading from active recall? How might we track which topics are actually weak vs. which feel weak?

✓ DT reframed the problem from "study more" to "study better and smarter"
📐
Step 2 — Analytical Thinking
Structure the causes

Frameworks used: MECE Issue Tree + 5 Whys + Cynefin

MECE Issue Tree (Level 1): Input quality | Study process | Assessment format | Physical/cognitive state. Each branch is ME (no overlap) and CE (together they cover all possible drivers of score decline).

5 Whys on Study Process: Scores low → didn't retrieve key material → used passive re-reading → never learned active recall → assumed re-reading = studying. Root cause: a false study method assumption, never examined.

Cynefin check: This is a Complicated problem — cause-effect discoverable with expert input. Apply analytical frameworks; don't just experiment randomly.

✓ AT revealed the root cause: passive study method, not insufficient time
🔬
Step 3 — Critical Thinking
Validate the conclusion

Frameworks used: Paul-Elder + RED Model + Socratic Method

Assumptions to test: That study method is the primary driver (not sleep disruption, anxiety, course difficulty increase, or life events). What evidence do I actually have vs. what am I inferring?

Evidence check (RED): Dunlosky et al. (2013) confirmed active recall has the highest utility of all study techniques — this is primary, peer-reviewed evidence. Sleep disruption (balancing loop from Systems Thinking) is also a plausible cause — needs to be tested separately.

Conclusion (qualified): Switch to spaced active recall this week and track scores. Monitor sleep independently. Retest both hypotheses in 3 weeks with observable data.

✓ CT prevented premature action — conclusion is evidence-based, not assumed
What Each Skill Alone Would Have Missed
🔬 CT alone would have evaluated arguments rigorously — but evaluated arguments about the wrong problem ("study more").
📐 AT alone would have structured causes — but might have accepted the first plausible root cause without validating the evidence.
💡 DT alone would have generated empathetic solutions — but to a problem never analytically structured or logically validated.
🎯 All three together produced the right problem (DT), the right cause (AT), and a defensible, evidence-based intervention plan (CT). This is the Master Sequence in practice.

⭐ Improving Students' Learning With Effective Learning Techniques
Dunlosky, Rawson, Marsh, Nathan, Willingham · 2013 · Psychological Science in the Public Interest
Free

The most comprehensive review of learning techniques ever conducted. Evaluated 10 study strategies on 4 criteria across thousands of studies. Finding: Practice testing (active recall) and distributed practice (spaced repetition) are the only two techniques with high utility. Re-reading and highlighting score the lowest. The evidence base for Anki in the Tools section.

Why: The primary source for recommending active recall and spaced repetition throughout all 3 Learning Plans. Read before choosing your study method.

32 key terms used throughout this guide — alphabetical order. Each entry notes which framework or foundation uses it most directly.
Abductive Reasoning

Inference to the best explanation from incomplete data. Used in diagnosis and investigation. Distinct from deductive (certainty) and inductive (probability).

→ Foundation 02: Logical Reasoning
Active Recall

Retrieving information from memory through testing rather than re-reading. The highest-utility study technique per Dunlosky et al. (2013). Implemented via Anki.

→ All Learning Plans · Tools: Anki
Balancing Loop

A feedback loop that resists change and regulates toward equilibrium. Example: a thermostat. Counteracts reinforcing loops. One of the two fundamental loop types in Systems Thinking.

→ FW12: Systems Thinking
CE (Collectively Exhaustive)

All branches of a decomposition together cover 100% of the problem space. The CE test: do all branches together account for everything? If there are gaps — restructure.

→ FW07: MECE + Issue Trees
Cognitive Bias

Systematic error in thinking that distorts decisions. Cannot be eliminated — but can be named and structurally reduced. Key types: Confirmation Bias, Anchoring, Dunning-Kruger, Sunk Cost Fallacy, Halo Effect, Fundamental Attribution Error, In-Group Bias, Availability Heuristic.

→ Foundation 03 · FW05 Six Hats · FW06 OODA Orient
Crazy 8s

GV Design Sprint ideation technique: fold paper into 8 panels, sketch 8 distinct concepts in 8 minutes (1 min each). Forces quantity over quality. The 8th idea is consistently the most original.

→ FW17: GV Design Sprint (Tuesday)
Cynefin (kuh-NEV-in)

Welsh for "habitat." Dave Snowden's 5-domain sense-making framework: Clear, Complicated, Complex, Chaotic, Disorder. Most dangerous error: applying Complicated-domain tools to Complex problems.

→ FW13 · Foundation 07: Systems Awareness
Deliberate Practice

Anders Ericsson: focused practice just beyond current ability, with immediate feedback and targeted correction. The primary driver of expert performance across all domains. Distinct from mere repetition by difficulty and intentionality.

→ All Learning Plans · Foundation 04: Growth Mindset
Divergent / Convergent Thinking

Two complementary cognitive modes. Divergent: generate many possibilities without evaluation (brainstorming, SCAMPER, Crazy 8s). Convergent: evaluate and narrow to the best option. The Double Diamond explicitly alternates between these in each diamond. Most thinking failures occur by converging too early.

→ FW15: Double Diamond · FW17: GV Design Sprint · FW18: SCAMPER
Empathy Map

A user research synthesis tool that captures what a user Says, Thinks, Does, and Feels. Exposes contradictions between stated preferences (Says) and actual behaviour (Does). Core tool in the Empathise stage of d.school HCD.

→ FW14: d.school HCD (Empathise) · Foundation 09: Active Observation
Feynman Technique

Explain a concept in plain language to a non-expert. Any gap reveals a gap in understanding. The most reliable test of genuine comprehension vs. familiarity.

→ 12-Month Plan Month 11 · All plans: Teaching milestone
Fermi Estimation

Breaking an unknown quantity into estimable components to arrive at a reasonable approximation. Trains quantitative fluency without requiring precise data. Named after physicist Enrico Fermi.

→ Foundation 06: Quantitative Literacy
Fishbone (Ishikawa) Diagram

Visual tool mapping causes across 6M categories (Man, Machine, Material, Method, Measurement, Mother Nature) in a fishbone shape. Used before 5 Whys to ensure all causal branches are considered.

→ FW10: 5 Whys + Fishbone
Hill (IBM DT)

IBM Enterprise DT outcome statement: WHO can do WHAT so that WOW (measurable outcome). A Hill is a user outcome, not a feature. Testable: you can verify whether the user achieved it.

→ FW16: IBM Enterprise Design Thinking
HMW (How Might We)

"How" assumes solutions exist. "Might" removes pressure. "We" invites collaboration. Generate 20+ HMW questions from a POV statement before narrowing. Never evaluate during generation.

→ FW14: d.school HCD (Define stage)
Intellectual Humility

Calibrated confidence: accurately knowing what you know and what you don't. Intellectually humble thinkers update beliefs faster and forecast more accurately. This is epistemic precision, not self-doubt.

→ Foundation 08 · FW03: Socratic Method
Inversion

Working backwards from failure or the opposite of the desired outcome. Charlie Munger: "Tell me where I'm going to die, so I'll never go there." Instead of asking "how do I succeed?", ask "what would guarantee failure?" and avoid those things. A powerful complement to First Principles and Pre-Mortem thinking.

→ FW09: First Principles · FW05: Six Hats (Black Hat)
Leverage Point

In Systems Thinking, the place where a small change produces the largest lasting effect. Donella Meadows: changing the goal of a system is a higher leverage point than changing any of its flows.

→ FW12: Systems Thinking
ME (Mutually Exclusive)

Each item in a decomposition belongs to exactly one branch — no overlaps. ME test: can an item simultaneously fit into two branches? If yes, the structure fails ME and must be restructured.

→ FW07: MECE + Issue Trees
Metacognition

Thinking about your own thinking. John Flavell (Stanford, 1979): the highest-leverage cognitive skill, more predictive of academic success than IQ. The invisible layer beneath all 19 frameworks.

→ Foundation 01 · Underpins all 19 frameworks
Mental Model

An internal representation of how something works. All decision-making is filtered through mental models (OODA's Orient phase). Models simplify reality, which is their power and their danger — an outdated or wrong mental model produces systematically wrong decisions. Updating mental models is the core skill of OODA and Systems Thinking.

→ FW06: OODA Loop (Orient) · FW12: Systems Thinking · Foundation 01: Metacognition
Pre-Mortem

Gary Klein (1998): before starting a project, imagine it has already failed — then work backwards to identify why. Counteracts optimism bias and groupthink more effectively than risk checklists. A powerful complement to Six Thinking Hats (Black Hat) and OODA's Orient audit.

→ FW05: Six Hats (Black Hat) · FW06: OODA · Foundation 03: Cognitive Biases
OODA Orient Phase

The most critical step of the OODA Loop. All observations are filtered through Orient — your mental models, experience, biases, and cultural tradition. Most strategic errors occur in Orient, not Decide.

→ FW06: OODA Loop
PESTEL

Political, Economic, Social, Technological, Environmental, Legal. Macro-environment scanner. Used as input to SWOT — PESTEL generates the Opportunities and Threats quadrants. Never use SWOT without PESTEL first.

→ FW11: SWOT + PESTEL
POV Statement

Point of View. d.school format: "[User] needs [need] because [insight]." The "because" forces a deep motivation, not a surface request. A validated POV is the output of the Define stage.

→ FW14: d.school HCD (Define)
Reinforcing Loop

A feedback loop where change amplifies itself — either virtuous (positive) or vicious (negative). Example: confidence → better performance → more confidence. Left unchecked, reinforcing loops create runaway growth or collapse.

→ FW12: Systems Thinking
Safe-to-Fail Experiment

In Cynefin's Complex domain: a small, reversible probe to generate learning rather than implement a solution. If it fails, the cost is acceptable. The appropriate response to complexity is probing, not planning.

→ FW13: Cynefin (Complex domain)
SCR (Situation-Complication-Resolution)

Barbara Minto's narrative template: Situation (known context) → Complication (tension/change) → Resolution (recommendation — always stated first). SCR is the frame; the Pyramid is the supporting structure.

→ FW08: Pyramid Principle
Second-Order Thinking

Asking "and then what?" — considering the consequences of consequences. First-order thinking solves the immediate problem; second-order thinking anticipates the ripple effects of the solution. Most unintended consequences emerge from first-order-only thinking. Central to Systems Thinking (feedback loops) and Cynefin (Complex domain).

→ FW12: Systems Thinking · FW13: Cynefin · Foundation 07: Systems Awareness
SIFT Method

Stop — Investigate the source — Find better coverage — Trace claims to original. Rapid information literacy protocol for evaluating online sources. Developed by Michael Caulfield, Washington State University.

→ Foundation 05: Information Literacy
Steelmanning

Constructing the strongest, most charitable version of an opposing argument before evaluating or dismissing it. The opposite of strawmanning. Required in Socratic Method Step 4 (Questioning Viewpoints).

→ Foundation 08 · FW03: Socratic Method · FW04: RED Model
